1. Bottom Line

Every dollar you convert today buys ~€0.876 — a modest improvement from Friday, but driven by euro-specific softness rather than broad dollar strength: the Dollar Index (DXY) barely moved (-0.04% to 100.73) while EUR/USD fell further (to 1.1420) on a sharp spike in European natural gas prices tied to the Strait of Hormuz conflict. The broader trend since February remains solidly in your favor; with the ECB (Jul 23) and Fed (Jul 28–29) both deciding within the next two weeks, this remains a reasonable window to convert a moderate tranche now and stage the rest around those two events.
  • Six months ago (Feb) $1 got you as little as roughly €0.8455 on average. As of today's spot, $1 gets you €0.8757 — comfortably above the 6-month average, edging further above Friday's €0.8747, though still short of June 24's high of €0.8806.
  • Today's move is a euro-specific move, not a broad-dollar one: EUR/USD eased to 1.1420 from 1.1433 (dollar stronger against the euro specifically) even as the Dollar Index was essentially flat at 100.73 (-0.04% d/d) — a divergence from Friday, when the two measures moved together.
  • The likely driver: European natural gas (TTF) spiked +4.08% today to €59.83/MWh (+29% month-to-date), tied directly to Strait of Hormuz-driven LNG supply concerns — a fresh euro-area-specific cost shock that is weighing on the euro independent of the dollar's own trajectory.
  • The Strait of Hormuz conflict remains active and unresolved, with no confirmed ceasefire as of today. This remains the single biggest wildcard for both currencies over the next two weeks — it is now visibly showing up in European energy prices, which is a new transmission channel worth watching closely.

3. What's Driving It

  • Euro-specific softness (today's dominant factor): EUR/USD eased to 1.1420 (-0.17% d/d) while the DXY held essentially flat at 100.73 (-0.04% d/d) — today's € per $1 gain is a euro story, not a dollar story, a change from Friday when the two moved in lockstep. 🟢 Favorable, today's dominant factor.
  • European gas price shock: TTF natural gas jumped +4.08% to €59.83/MWh today and is up +29% month-to-date, driven by Strait of Hormuz-linked LNG supply concerns and stronger power-generation demand — a fresh euro-area-specific cost pressure that plausibly explains today's divergence from the broad dollar. 🟢 Favorable while it persists, but a genuinely new variable to track.
  • Fed vs. ECB rate gap (still wide, still structurally favorable): Fed holds at 3.50–3.75%, next decision Jul 28–29; ECB has held its deposit rate at 2.25% since its June hike, with markets pricing a high probability of no change at the Jul 23 decision, though longer-dated pricing has shifted toward roughly a 70% chance of a September hike — itself a euro-supportive signal that could cap further € per $1 gains once priced in. 🟢 Favorable near-term, watch for the September repricing.
  • Strait of Hormuz — active conflict, still unresolved: No confirmed ceasefire; the conflict is now visibly transmitting into European energy prices rather than just shipping and safe-haven flows. A classic two-sided risk: further escalation likely keeps pressuring European costs (favorable for € per $1 via euro softness, oddly enough) while a sudden ceasefire could reverse both the gas spike and today's move quickly. 🟢/🟡 Favorable while active, but capable of reversing fast.

APEX Take — Conversion Strategy

€ per $1 edged up to €0.8757 today (+0.11%), extending the favorable trend, but on a different driver than Friday — euro-specific softness tied to a sharp European gas-price spike (+29% MTD) rather than broad dollar strength, which actually stayed flat. This is a new transmission channel from the Strait of Hormuz conflict worth watching: it's currently working in your favor, but a ceasefire that unwinds the gas spike could reverse it quickly. You're still getting meaningfully more euro per dollar than you were in February, and today's rate remains a reasonable entry point.

Recommendation: Convert a moderate portion now (e.g., 35–40%) at ~€0.876 per dollar to lock in a rate above the 6-month average, and hold the remainder to convert after the July 23 ECB decision and July 28–29 FOMC meeting — both fall inside the next two weeks and carry real potential to move the rate meaningfully in either direction given the still-wide Fed-ECB rate gap and an unresolved, now energy-market-impacting Hormuz conflict. If your bank or transfer provider offers a forward contract or rate alert/limit order, that remains a better tool than manual timing for the remaining balance.
