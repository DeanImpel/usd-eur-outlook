1. Bottom Line

Every dollar you convert today buys ~€0.875 — up for the first day after a two-day pullback, as the broad dollar firmed (DXY +0.16% to 100.78) on Fed-caution positioning ahead of the July 28–29 FOMC decision. The broader trend since February remains solidly in your favor; with the ECB (Jul 23) and Fed (Jul 28–29) both deciding within the next two weeks, this is a reasonable window to convert a moderate tranche now and stage the rest around those two events.
  • Six months ago (Feb) $1 got you as little as roughly €0.8455 on average. As of today's spot, $1 gets you €0.8747 — comfortably above the 6-month average, and back above where it stood two days ago, even after pulling back from June 24's high of €0.8806.
  • Today's move is a broad-dollar move, not an EUR-specific one: EUR/USD eased to 1.1433 from 1.1464 (dollar stronger against the euro), tracking the Dollar Index (DXY), which rose +0.16% to 100.78 — the two measures are moving together today, unlike yesterday's divergence.
  • The Strait of Hormuz conflict remains active and, per overnight reporting, continues to escalate: traceable large-vessel crossings via the Oman-hugging route have been at a standstill since Tuesday, and an estimated 6,000 seafarers remain stranded on vessels that used to transit at ~130/day. This remains the single biggest wildcard for both the dollar and the euro over the next two weeks, and today's broad dollar firmness suggests some of that risk-off demand may be starting to land in the dollar more broadly, not just other safe havens.

3. What's Driving It

  • Broad dollar firming (today's dominant factor): DXY rose +0.16% to 100.78, tracking Fed-caution positioning ahead of the Jul 28–29 FOMC meeting; EUR/USD eased in step to 1.1433, pushing € per $1 up to €0.8747. Unlike yesterday, the EUR pair and the broad dollar index moved together today. 🟢 Favorable, today's dominant factor.
  • Fed vs. ECB rate gap (still wide, still structurally favorable): Fed holds at 3.50–3.75%, next decision Jul 28–29; ECB has held its deposit rate at 2.25% since its June hike, with markets pricing a 93% probability of no change at the Jul 23 decision. The gap remains roughly ~125–150bp in the dollar's favor — a structural tailwind for more euro per dollar over time. 🟢 Favorable, structural.
  • Strait of Hormuz — active conflict, still escalating: Fighting between US and Iranian forces continued overnight; traceable Oman-lane crossings have been halted since Tuesday and roughly 6,000 seafarers remain stranded. A classic safe-haven catalyst that would normally support the dollar broadly — today's DXY move suggests that may be starting to happen, though it remains a two-sided risk if a ceasefire emerges suddenly. 🟢/🟡 Favorable while active, but capable of reversing fast.
  • Countervailing risk: A dovish FOMC pivot on rate-cut timing (Jul 28–29), a hawkish ECB surprise (Jul 23), or a sudden Hormuz de-escalation/ceasefire could each reverse today's gain quickly. Watch both event dates closely. 🔴 Watch.

APEX Take — Conversion Strategy

€ per $1 bounced back to €0.8747 today (+0.29%), ending a two-day pullback, on a broad-dollar firming move tied to Fed-caution positioning rather than an EUR-specific swing — DXY and EUR/USD moved together for the first time in a few days. The Strait of Hormuz conflict remains active and shows no sign of near-term resolution, which continues to keep a risk premium in play for both currencies. You're still getting meaningfully more euro per dollar than you were in February, and today's bounce is a reasonable entry point.

Recommendation: Convert a moderate portion now (e.g., 35–40%) at ~€0.875 per dollar to lock in a rate above the 6-month average, and hold the remainder to convert after the July 23 ECB decision and July 28–29 FOMC meeting — both fall inside the next two weeks and carry real potential to move the rate meaningfully in either direction given the still-wide Fed-ECB rate gap and an unresolved, active Hormuz conflict. If your bank or transfer provider offers a forward contract or rate alert/limit order, that remains a better tool than manual timing for the remaining balance.
