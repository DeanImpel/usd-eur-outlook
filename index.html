1. Bottom Line

Every dollar you convert today buys ~€0.876 — down slightly from yesterday, as a cooler-than-expected June CPI print reversed part of the dollar's Hormuz-driven safe-haven strength. The favorable multi-day streak broke today, but the broader trend since February remains in your favor; with the ECB (Jul 23) and Fed (Jul 28–29) both deciding within the next two weeks, this is a reasonable moment to convert a smaller tranche now and wait for more clarity before committing the rest.
  • Six months ago (Feb) $1 got you as little as roughly €0.8455 on average. As of today's spot, $1 gets you €0.8756 — still well above the 6-month average, even after today's pullback from June 24's high of €0.8806.
  • Today's move is driven by dollar weakness, not euro strength: June CPI came in at 3.5% YoY, cooler than the 3.8% forecast and down from 4.2% in May, reducing near-term Fed rate-hike expectations and pulling the Dollar Index down to 100.79 (a second straight losing session).
  • Notably, this happened despite the Strait of Hormuz crisis escalating further — the U.S. naval blockade of Iranian ports resumed Jul 14, and Trump proposed a 20% security toll on Hormuz cargo Jul 13. Normally that combination drives dollar strength; today the cooler CPI print dominated instead, a reminder that macro data can override geopolitical risk premiums on any given day.

3. What's Driving It

  • June CPI reaction (new, dominant today): Headline CPI printed 3.5% YoY — cooler than the 3.8% consensus forecast and down from 4.2% in May — reviving modest Fed rate-cut expectations and pulling the dollar lower across the board. This is the single biggest driver of today's € per $1 pullback. 🔴 Unfavorable, today's dominant factor.
  • Dollar Index (DXY) weakness: Fell to 100.79 today, down from 101.29 yesterday and below the 101 level for the first time in several sessions — a direct read-through of the cooler CPI print. 🔴 Unfavorable.
  • Fed vs. ECB rate gap (still wide, still favorable): Fed holds at 3.50–3.75%; ECB has held its deposit rate at 2.25% since the June 11 hike. The gap remains roughly ~125–150bp in the dollar's favor even after today's CPI-driven repricing — the structural tailwind hasn't disappeared, just cooled off. 🟢 Still favorable, less acute.
  • Strait of Hormuz — blockade resumed, escalation continues: The U.S. naval blockade of Iranian ports resumed at 4pm ET Jul 14, the fifth day of the current escalation phase (which began after Iran allegedly struck commercial vessels Jul 8 and retaliated against U.S. bases in Kuwait, Bahrain, and Jordan). Trump has proposed a 20% security toll on all Hormuz cargo. Traffic has collapsed to as few as 22 ships crossing Jul 9, versus 147 pre-war. This remains a live safe-haven catalyst that simply didn't dominate today's price action. 🟡 Two-sided — bullish for the dollar if it re-escalates further or spooks markets, but already partially priced in after months of conflict.
  • Countervailing risk: If incoming data continues to run cooler (supporting Fed rate-cut bets), or if the Hormuz situation de-escalates unexpectedly, € per $1 could give back more of its 6-month gain. Conversely, a hawkish FOMC hold on Jul 28–29 or a fresh Hormuz escalation could reverse today's move quickly. 🔴 Watch both directions.

APEX Take — Conversion Strategy

€ per $1 slipped to €0.8756 today (-0.23%), the first pullback in several sessions, as a cooler June CPI print outweighed the safe-haven pull from the resumed Hormuz blockade. The broader picture hasn't changed — you're still getting meaningfully more euro per dollar than you were in February — but today's reversal is a reminder that this rate can move against you quickly on a single data surprise.

Recommendation: Convert a smaller portion now (e.g., 25–30%) at ~€0.876 per dollar to stay ahead of any further slippage, and hold the majority to convert after the July 23 ECB decision and July 28–29 FOMC meeting — both fall inside the next two weeks and are more likely than not to reassert the dollar-favorable trend given the still-wide Fed-ECB rate gap and an unresolved Hormuz crisis. If your bank or transfer provider offers a forward contract or rate alert/limit order, that remains a better tool than manual timing for the remaining balance.
